About the Workshop

Overview & Motivation

Over the past decade, deep learning has demonstrated remarkable effectiveness across many domains, and robotics has likewise begun to embrace deep neural models for learning sophisticated perception modules and control policies. Yet deploying such models in real robotic systems exposes several persistent limitations.

Neural networks excel at pattern recognition but fall short at structured, logic-based reasoning. The lack of transparent, human-interpretable mechanisms is a major concern when these models are intended for safety-critical, real-world use. Knowledge learned by deep models is difficult to disentangle and reuse across tasks; and because neural decision boundaries are opaque, predicting failure cases remains hard.

In contrast, humans rely heavily on abstraction and symbolic reasoning — abilities that enable generalization, high-level cognition, and the manipulation of conceptual structures. Neuro-Symbolic AI has therefore emerged as a promising middle ground, fusing the representational power of neural networks with the compositional and interpretable nature of symbolic reasoning.

Reasoning

Neural networks excel at pattern recognition but struggle with structured, logic-based inference over discrete symbolic representations.

Explainability

Understanding why a deep model behaves a certain way remains difficult—a major concern for safety-critical deployments.

Modularity

Knowledge learned by deep models is hard to disentangle and systematically reapply across different tasks and contexts.

Robustness

Deep models remain vulnerable to adversarial and naturally occurring perceptual distortions, with no general defenses available yet.

Research Agenda

Discussion Topics

We will explicitly encourage speakers to engage with the following open questions, focusing on current challenges and the bottlenecks that prevent scaling to human-level symbolic reasoning.

  • 01 How are symbols represented in neural networks — distributed in subnetwork activations or encoded with single neurons?
  • 02 Should deep neural networks be designed to produce symbols and rules directly? What are the design principles, and is such engineering scalable?
  • 03 Should symbols emerge from robot–environment interactions (grounding), or should they be pre-designed and grounded via learning?
  • 04 How scalable is it to pre-design symbols and rules, and later learn the grounding via deep networks?
  • 05 How can multiple robotic cognitive capabilities — reasoning, planning, communication, and language — be integrated with perception and control?
  • 06 How should symbolic representations of time be handled in long-horizon tasks, curriculum learning, or developmental robotic systems?

Call for Contributions

Submit Your Work

We invite submissions from researchers working at the intersection of neural and symbolic approaches in robotics and AI. Accepted contributions will be presented as oral talks (5–15 minutes) and/or posters, depending on quality assessed by reviewers.

Who Should Attend & Submit?

We aim to draw a diverse, interdisciplinary audience. We strongly encourage submissions from underrepresented researchers and communities, and the review process will consider gender, race, ethnicity, and geographic balance.

  • Cognitive and developmental roboticists working on symbol grounding and emergence
  • Robotics researchers working on symbolic AI who wish to incorporate recent deep learning advances
  • Robotics researchers working on deep learning who want to leverage symbolic reasoning and explainability
  • Researchers in vision and NLP working at the neural–symbolic intersection

The organizing team spans multiple continents and institutions specifically to ensure diversity of reach — we welcome participants who do not traditionally attend ICDL.
